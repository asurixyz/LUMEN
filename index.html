Maritime Trade & Investment : Observer’s Overview

Maritime Corridors and Power

Maritime corridors are where economic and strategic interests converge. Sea lanes carry energy and goods while also reflecting patterns of alignment, dependence, and leverage. Trade volumes and investment flows provide signals of influence and long-term strategy.

South China Sea

The South China Sea is a major choke point for global shipping and energy transit. Overlapping claims make it a contested zone. Chinese investments in ports, energy infrastructure, and Belt and Road projects combine economic integration with strategic positioning. Tracking trade shares, energy exposure, and annual capital commitments helps assess shifting power.

Bay of Bengal

The Bay of Bengal links the Indian Ocean with Southeast Asia. Trade growth here mirrors adjustments in global supply chains. Investments in ports, special economic zones, and logistics corridors mark competition for connectivity. India, China, and external partners use these projects to balance each other’s influence.

East African Coast

The East African coast is a rising frontier for trade and blue-economy growth. Expanding port and corridor projects connect maritime routes to inland markets. The pace and sectoral focus of investments reveal which actors are anchoring long-term influence.

Persian Gulf

The Persian Gulf remains both an energy hub and a source of financial capital. Trade flows show exposure to global price cycles and political risks, while sustained investments suggest deeper interdependence that extends beyond hydrocarbons.
